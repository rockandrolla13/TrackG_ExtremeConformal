\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Paragraph 1: Problem statement — sample size vs confidence level
Financial risk regulation demands prediction intervals at extreme confidence levels. Basel III requires Value-at-Risk at the 99th percentile; stress testing mandates coverage at 99.9\% and beyond—the once-per-decade events that determine institutional solvency. Conformal prediction constructs distribution-free prediction intervals with guaranteed coverage: given $n$ calibration observations, the interval $\hat{C}_{1-\alpha}$ satisfies $\Prob(Y_{n+1} \in \hat{C}_{1-\alpha}) \geq 1-\alpha$ under exchangeability. The method is elegant: rank the test point's conformity score among calibration scores, and include all responses whose scores fall below the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile. But this elegance conceals a fundamental limitation. The empirical $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $n$ observations is undefined when $\alpha < 1/(n+1)$. With 1{,}000 calibration points, the 99.9th percentile cannot be estimated—the conformal interval defaults to the entire real line. Valid coverage, but operationally worthless. This is not a finite-sample nuisance that vanishes asymptotically; it is a structural mismatch between sample size and confidence level. Regulators requiring 99.9\% coverage need either 10{,}000+ calibration observations or a principled method to extrapolate beyond the empirical range.

% Paragraph 2: Literature position — EVT exists, conformal exists, but not combined for finance
Extreme value theory (EVT) provides exactly this extrapolation. The Pickands-Balkema-de Haan theorem establishes that exceedances above a high threshold follow a Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD), enabling estimation of arbitrary tail quantiles from the subset of observations exceeding the threshold. A sample of 1{,}000 observations with 50 threshold exceedances can reliably estimate the 99.9th percentile via GPD fitting, even though the empirical quantile is undefined. \citet{pasche2025extreme} recently connected EVT and conformal prediction, applying GPD extrapolation to conformity scores for environmental extremes—floods, wildfires, heatwaves. Their method constructs conservative prediction intervals at confidence levels far beyond what empirical calibration can reach. However, their framework assumes exchangeability of calibration and test data, appropriate for environmental forecasting with seasonal adjustment but violated by the serial dependence inherent in financial returns. Flash crashes, liquidity crises, and market dislocations—precisely the tail events financial regulators care about—occur in temporally dependent sequences, not exchangeable samples.

% Paragraph 3: Our contribution — GPD extrapolation under mixing
We extend extreme conformal prediction to temporally dependent data. The key observation is that GPD extrapolation and coverage guarantees under mixing address orthogonal problems: GPD solves the sample-size-vs-confidence mismatch by estimating tail quantiles from threshold exceedances; mixing corrections bound how much serial dependence degrades coverage. Under $\beta$-mixing—the standard dependence condition for financial returns—we prove that the coverage gap decomposes additively:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:intro-bound}
\left| \Prob(Y_{n+1} \in \hat{C}_{1-\alpha}^{\mathrm{GPD}}) - (1-\alpha) \right| \leq \underbrace{C_1 \cdot \beta(\tau)}_{\text{mixing}} + \underbrace{C_2 \cdot n_u^{-1/2}}_{\text{GPD estimation}},
\end{equation}
where $\beta(\tau)$ is the mixing coefficient at lag $\tau$, $n_u$ is the number of threshold exceedances used for GPD fitting, and $C_1, C_2$ are explicit constants. For high-frequency financial data with thousands of daily observations, both terms are small. The bound provides what practitioners need: finite-width prediction intervals at 99.9\%+ confidence with quantified coverage guarantees under realistic dependence.

% Paragraph 4: Circuit breaker censoring — unique financial challenge
A complication specific to financial markets requires attention. Exchange circuit breakers halt trading when prices move beyond specified limits, censoring the true extremes we seek to model. The May 6, 2010 flash crash saw the Dow Jones Industrial Average drop 1{,}000 points before trading halts intervened; the Swiss National Bank's 2015 peg removal moved EUR/CHF by 30\% in minutes before liquidity evaporated. These censored observations are not missing at random—they represent the largest moves, exactly the tail we need to estimate. Naive GPD fitting treats circuit breaker prices as true extremes, biasing tail estimates downward. We incorporate a truncation correction into the GPD likelihood, treating halted prices as right-censored observations. This correction is essential for any EVT analysis where trading halts occur, yet prior conformal prediction work has not addressed it.

% Paragraph 5: Impossibility — light tails don't need extrapolation
We establish a converse result delimiting when extreme conformal prediction is necessary. For distributions with exponentially decaying tails—where the tail index $\xi \leq 0$—the GPD extrapolation provides no advantage over conservative confidence intervals around the empirical maximum. Intuitively, light-tailed distributions concentrate mass away from extremes; the empirical maximum is already a reliable bound. Heavy-tailed distributions ($\xi > 0$), by contrast, have unbounded support and slowly decaying tail probabilities; here GPD extrapolation is essential for finite-width intervals. Financial returns are heavy-tailed \citep{cont2001empirical}, placing them squarely in the regime where our method applies. This impossibility result is not about dependence structure—it is about tail shape.

% Paragraph 6: Contributions
We make four contributions. First, we develop \emph{extreme conformal prediction under mixing}, combining GPD tail extrapolation with coverage guarantees for $\beta$-mixing processes. The coverage bound~\eqref{eq:intro-bound} decomposes into mixing and estimation components, both explicit and computable. Second, we introduce a censoring correction for circuit breaker effects, extending the GPD likelihood to handle truncated extremes common in financial data. Third, we prove an impossibility result: for light-tailed distributions, GPD extrapolation cannot improve on empirical bounds, establishing that our method targets heavy-tailed processes specifically. Fourth, we validate the approach on documented financial tail events: FX flash crashes (SNB peg removal 2015, Brexit 2016, JPY flash crash 2019) and cryptocurrency collapses (COVID March 2020, FTX November 2022). Across settings, extreme conformal prediction achieves 99.9\% coverage while providing intervals 40--60\% narrower than classical conformal methods that must span the observed range.

\paragraph{Related work.}
The methodological foundation is extreme value theory, specifically the peaks-over-threshold approach \citep{pickands1975statistical,balkema1974residual}. For financial applications, \citet{mcneil2000estimation} establish GPD-based VaR estimation, and \citet{embrechts2013modelling} provide comprehensive treatment of EVT in risk management. Our conformalization of GPD extrapolation draws on \citet{pasche2025extreme}, who apply this idea to environmental extremes under exchangeability; we extend their framework to dependent data.

Conformal prediction for time series is surveyed by \citet{zaffran2022adaptive} and \citet{xu2023sequential}. Coverage under mixing is established by \citet{barber2025predictive}, whose bounds we adapt for our setting. Conformalized quantile regression \citep{romano2019conformalized} is the standard baseline for interval estimation but inherits the sample-size limitation at extreme quantiles. \citet{vovk2012conditional} establishes that exact conditional coverage is impossible without distributional assumptions; our guarantees are marginal. Adaptive methods \citep{gibbs2021adaptive} adjust coverage over time but do not address extreme quantile estimation.

For financial risk, \citet{schmitt2026taming} develop regime-weighted conformal calibration for VaR at moderate quantiles (95--99\%); their approach relies on empirical quantiles and cannot reach 99.9\%+. The circuit breaker literature \citep{subrahmanyam1994circuit,kim2008price} documents price limit effects but not in the context of tail estimation or conformal prediction.
